Immunotherapy with dendritic cells

The immune system helps people stay healthy by recognizing pathogens such as bacteria and viruses, as well as cancer cells, as harmful and killing them. It has been known for some time that the so-called antigen-presenting cells are necessary for the recognition of foreign bodies (antigens). These antigen-presenting cells inform the immune system of the invasion and activate other immune cells to fight it in a variety of ways.
Dendritic cells are among the most important antigen-presenting cells. They have treelike cell expansions (Greek Dendron = tree), with which they look for foreign bodies on their way through the lymph and blood.
Cancer cells are different from healthy cells. This difference, this amount of "foreign" is used in immunotherapy to fight cancer using the body's own defenses.

In addition to the ability to isolate small amounts of dendritic cells from blood or bone marrow, they can be obtained from their precursor cells in the blood, monocytes. Only the elucidation of their origin from the myeloid series made it possible to obtain large numbers of antigen-presenting cells in a simple way (Peters et al. 1987).

In dendritic cell immunotherapy, a subset of the patient's white blood cells, monocytes, is obtained by routine blood sampling or leukaphoresis. In the laboratory these monocytes differentiate into antigen-presenting cells, dendritic cells of monocytes, using cell messenger substances (cytokines, growth factors).

During this time, still "immature" dendritic cells can also learn about cancer. Immature dendritic cells can absorb tumor antigens and tumor lysates, phagocytose and thus present important tumor features to the immune system on their surface. This process is called priming. In addition, dendritic cells are also capable of absorbing tumor antigens in the patient's body and can be activated using cell messenger substances.

Depending on the individual situation of the patient and the characteristics of his tumor, the stimulation protocols are determined individually for each patient.

After 7 days in cell culture, "trained" and activated dendritic cells are returned to the patient as a vaccine into the skin or vein. Concomitant immunological stimulation with cellular messenger substances can be carried out locally, since in some cases this can enhance the immune response.

Treatment process

Before starting therapy, an updated serology of infection for HIV, hepatitis B and C must be performed. Even if the infection status is known, we are obliged to retest it shortly before starting therapy in a certified laboratory, which has been reported to our regulatory authorities for reasons of product safety and occupational safety ...
After obtaining monocytes, they are cultured in the laboratory according to various protocols and differentiated into dendritic cells or tolerogenic or regenerating macrophages. Depending on the protocol, patients receive their "trained" cells back in 4-7 days.
Treatment is usually given as a baseline immunization three times, one day apart, for one week.

Rehabilitation after cancer

Cancer rehabilitation is aimed at Patients who have completed primary cancer treatment and who wish to improve their physical and mental health. Each victim responds differently to the cancer problem and its treatment. Thus, in a holistic and interdisciplinary approach, treatment goals are jointly defined in order to gain new strength to return to professional and personal daily life.
